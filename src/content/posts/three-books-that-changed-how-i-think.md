I'm wary of "books that changed my life" lists. They tend toward the performative. But there are a few books that genuinely shifted something in how I approach problems, and I think they're worth sharing.

## *Gödel, Escher, Bach* — Douglas Hofstadter

The obvious one. I read it at seventeen and understood perhaps a third of it. I re-read it at twenty-three and understood maybe half. The point isn't to understand all of it — it's to get comfortable with the idea that **self-reference and recursion are everywhere**, and that formal systems have limits that are themselves deeply interesting.

It's also the book that made me realise that the boundaries between disciplines are mostly administrative.

## *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* — Thomas Kuhn

I came to Kuhn late, after already having spent a few years doing research. That was probably the right time. Reading it as a practising scientist rather than as a philosophy student meant I could see the paradigm shifts he describes not as abstract history but as something I might be living through.

The key insight for me wasn't about paradigm shifts themselves, but about **normal science** — the idea that most scientific work is puzzle-solving within an accepted framework, and that this is fine. Not everything needs to be revolutionary. Most progress is incremental, and that's valuable.

## *Thinking, Fast and Slow* — Daniel Kahneman

This one is almost a cliché at this point, but I don't care. Kahneman's distinction between System 1 and System 2 thinking gave me a vocabulary for something I'd been experiencing but couldn't articulate: the difference between intuitive leaps and careful reasoning, and the ways each can go wrong.

As a researcher, I find myself constantly negotiating between the two. You need intuition to know which problems are worth working on. You need rigour to actually solve them. The trick is knowing which mode you're in.

---

I'm sure this list will grow. But three feels like an honest number for now.
